# User feedback — interview notes

**Method:** 5 short interviews with potential users (team members + managers of small organizations). Illustrative verbatims for the discovery.

---

## Persona A — The team member who executes

**Léa, designer at a 20-person agency**

> "Honestly, task tools — I open them twice and I quit. It's always too much. I just want to see what I have to do today, that's it."

> "What kills me is having to fill in fields everywhere. Status, priority, label, estimate… I just want to check it off when it's done."

> "If it takes me more than 5 seconds to add a task, I won't do it. I go back to sticky notes."

**Takeaway:** speed of use > richness. Friction kills adoption. The "check it off when done" gesture must be instant and satisfying.

---

## Persona B — The manager who steers

**Marc, COO of a 25-person studio**

> "My problem isn't the tasks, it's visibility. On Monday I want to know where we stand without walking around the desks."

> "I don't need to control everything. Just a barometer: are we moving forward, are we behind, how much did we close this week."

> "A simple chart is enough for me. If I see the line going up, I'm reassured. If it flattens, I know I need to go check."

**Takeaway:** the manager doesn't want to micro-manage, they want a **barometer**. A visual overview (counters + a progress line) covers 80% of their need.

---

## Tension between the two personas

- **Léa** wants the least screen possible, zero reporting, just her tasks.
- **Marc** wants a steering view.

→ The product must serve both **without imposing the weight of one on the other**: the team keeps an ultra-simple interface (just the tasks), the manager gets a separate view (the dashboard) that feeds automatically from what the team does, with no extra effort from them.

## Central insight

The data in the manager's dashboard must be a **free by-product** of the team's normal use. If the team has to "fill in" anything to feed the manager's reporting, it's dead. The manager sees progress **because** the team checks off its tasks — nothing more.
